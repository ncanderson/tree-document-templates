Fagus Grandifolia

(American Beech)

Native

Leaves: Alternate, sharp single serrated margins, pinnate veination

Beech nut prickly and typically 2 seeded

Buds: long pointed and inbricate scales

Bark

Fagus Grandifolia is a native forest tree often seen on western slopes with oaks.

It prefers dry, acidic, soil and is salt tolerant. Fagus Grandifolia cannot take soil compaction

Landscape Notes: can be used as a hedge when planted 12’’ to 14’’ on center. Thin skin requires a mulch ring 1’’-2’’ deep

Acer Rubrum

(red maple)

Native

Leaves: Opposite, deep sinuses, serrated margins, 3 lobes, palmate veination

Buds: Clusters of slightly rounded and reddish buds

Bark

Acer Rubrum is a native tree that thrives in both wet lowlands and dry and rocky highlands. It can withstand drought, soil compaction, and salt.

Landscape Notes: can be 40-60ft in height, great shade tree. Root system is exposed so do not plant grass around it, ring with mulch. Do not buy grafted maple.

Acer Griseum              

(paper bark maple)

Non Native

Leaves: Opposite, compound leaf with leaflets, palmately compound, and slightly pubescent

Buds:

Bark: Ornamental tree known for its distinctive bark

Acer Griseum is a small tree used for ornamental purposes. It prefers well drained, slightly moist, acidic soil. It is not salt tolerant

Acer Saccharum

(sugar maple)

Native

Leaves: Opposite leaves with 5 lobes per leaf. Deeply serrated leaves that exude a clear sap from the petiole when detached

Buds: Tiny and sharply pointed

Bark

Acer Saccharum is a wonderful shade tree that prefers moist but well drained slightly acidic soil. It has a high salt sensitivity.

Landscape Notes: very shallow roots, ring mulch

Quercus Palustris          

(Pin Oak)

Native

Leaves - deeply lobed, alternate leaves with 5-7 lobes. Leaves are pinnately veined. Small branchlets that look like small pins lend to its common name, Pin Oak.

Buds -

Bark -

Quercus palustris is a hardy tree that is native to wet, acidic soil. It is a very hardy tree that can get very large and is slightly salt tolerant. Quercus palustris makes a great street tree and specimen tree.

Syringa vulgaris    

(Common Lilac)

Non Native

Leaves - Leaves are cordate (heart shaped), matte, and entire. They are opposite and thick.

 

Buds -

The buds of the Syringa vulgaris are distinct because the terminal buds are paired.

Syringa vulgaris have a very distinct flower and seed pod that are easy to identify.

                       

Notes: Syringa vulgaris is a common landscape plant that makes a great hedgerow when planted 5ft on center. It is prone to powdery mildew when it is not planted with room for air circulation but is otherwise very hardy. It should be planted in well draining soil as it does not like it’s feet to remain wet.

Syringa villosa

(Late Lilac)

Non-Native

Leaves -

The leaves of the Syringa villosa are opposite and the margin is slightly wavy. They are slightly rough in comparison with the Syringa vulgaris (Common Lilac).

Bud -

The Bud of the Syringa villosa are a distinct identifying characteristic when compared with the other syringa varieties. Syringa villosa terminal buds have 2 lateral buds attached.

 

Notes: Syringa villosa prefers full sun

Quercus rubra      

(Red Oak)

Native

Leaves - Alternate leaves with larger leaves and lobes than other oaks.

Buds - clustered at terminus

Bark - The bark of the Quercus rubra has very straight furrows

Notes: Quercus rubra is a fast growing tree with a form that is tall, straight, and true. It prefers acidic soil and is often seen on dryer slopes.

Acer saccharinum  

(Silver Maple)

Native

Leaves - Acer saccharinum leaves are distinctly 5 lobed and silvery underneath. The lobes are deep and the leaves have an almost lacey appearance.

 

Bark - The bark is silverish in color and slightly orangish in the cracks

Notes: Acer sacharinum is native to the lowlands and thrives with a lot of moisture. They are weak limbed and need regular pruning and cableing.

Acer palmatum  

(Japanese Maple)

Non Native

Leaves - Deeply lobed, opposite leaves with toothed margins.

 

Notes: Acer palmatum is a marginally hearty tree commonly used in landscaping. It is not salt tolerant and needs to be protected from western sun and northwestern winds.
